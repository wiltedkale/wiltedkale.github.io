Childhood 1994-2003

1994 - I'm 4

I hated public restrooms but sometimes it's either that or shit your pants/explode so we pulled off the highway and into a gas station. My mom got the key from the foreign guy. I couldn't talk to people so I just followed her around, to the side of the building where there was an external door (bad sign). My mom unlocked the door and I walked in.

It sucks trying to work out your constipated turds in a room that was hit by a nuke, but this one was. Every public bathroom was so bad but this was one of the first ones—part of an early group of bathrooms that created a kind of trauma. I quickly developed a condition where I'd just hold it in for days, over a week sometimes, because I was too afraid to be that vulnerable. And when I finally did go it came out like a brick, millimeter by millimeter, like a submarine coming out of two couch cushions. It was very painful.

I ended up just peeing instead. I couldn't shit here. If I was too stressed or vulnerable the poo wouldn't come out. Same with urinals. I sucked at tests too.

It was like a locker room at a public pool. Only this one had rancid shit mixed in. And on the floor too. There was at least an inch of water on the floor in some parts, but other parts were somehow fine. There was toilet paper floating in the bad parts. I had to shit but maybe I needed to vomit. Maybe I would vomit shit. I hadn't even seen the worst, yet.

The toilet was the eye of the storm. Inside was an unflushable mass of TP and excrement that had risen to the seat. As my pee hit, it felt like me and the guy who left it there were touching. I felt betrayed by this guy, really. FLUSH. I had an image of this guy with long greasy hair and ugly teeth, selling drugs. I saw him with a switchblade, violently swinging it at people. At 4 I thought these were bad things.

The soap dispenser was empty so I just ran straight out of there. My mom returned the key, then checked out with 10 bucks of coffee and snacks—little Entenmann's shits we always ate, hydrogenated crapola. We belted up. She passed my brother the bag of crapola and we were back on route 95.

My mom tore open the package of a Devil Dog with her teeth, went hard into it. It looked like she was eating my shit.

* * *

I stared out the window and watched the cars. I started to see some repeats. My brother was in the front seat with his feet on the dash, shoes off, mashing the buttons on his Game Gear. His mouth hung half open as he kicked ass, or got his ass kicked, in Mortal Kombat II.

I wonder what my mom was thinking about. Stuff wasn't going so good on the money front and her friend Nancy was going to help, I guess. I just sat there with a runny nose and stared at the road lines, impressed by how fast they were going by. My brother sat up there with his Game Gear, squeaked little farts out, grunted, took a sip of his soda. This was my mom's problem, we were just along for the paid vacation. We were in Connecticut, I knew that.

My mom put on her blinker. We had seen so many people turn off while we kept going, now it was our turn. We turned off and everyone else kept going. I was glad because my ass and back hurt and I still needed to crap pretty bad. The commercial stuff was behind us, this new road was narrow and had tall trees on either side. It was late Autumn so only the pine needles were left. The yards were big and came all the way to the road. The houses were old.

My brother read off the directions for the final few turns. He was good at it, like he was leading us back home. We slowed down, leaned over to check the numbers on the mailbox. This was it. My mom turned the wheel and pulled our old Plymouth rustbucket down the long skinny driveway. When we got to the end there was a lady standing there.

* * *

I climbed out of the van, closed the slider door myself—pulled as hard as I could with both hands. The thing was a hundred pounds. We lined up and my mom introduced me and my brother to Nancy. Nancy introduced us to her son, Michael. Michael said hi. My brother said hi back. I didn't say anything.

Michael seemed alright from the start. He was soft spoken and kind. He loved video games. His mom could cook. He played video games and ate his mom's cooking. This was the circle of life.

While my mom talked with Nancy about grown up stuff, Michael led me and my brother into the basement. Older people didn't like the basement because it took effort to go down and come up again, and was generally shitty and uncomfortable, so it was a place for kids to go and play, hide out. It was cheap, infertile land. We used it.

Our moms' voices trailed off as they went one way and we went another. When we got to the bottom of the stairs we saw another kid, Jimmy, sitting on the carpet facing the TV. He was waiting for Michael to unpause the game. He was much more interested in the game than us. We just stood there breathing the basement must in, waiting for these dudes to show us how it's done. We didn't expect much. Michael pushed a button on his controller and the TV unfroze, and with it the music. The music was a jet engine. It sent us back 5 feet and dropped us on our ass.

I looked up: Michael and Jimmy were really digging in, ramming their jeeps into each other at full speed. Jimmy jammed into first but took the next ramp at an angle, flew off the track and exploded.

"DAMMIT," Jimmy fumed. It handed Michael the lead.

The game was beautiful, my eyes were glued to the screen. My brother's too.

"You guys never played Rock n' Roll Racing1?" asked Michael.

We hadn't, but why hadn't we. This was way better than anything we were doing before. It changed everything.

A flurry of missiles connected with Michael's car and it exploded.

"You wanna try?" he asked.

I saw Michael pass my brother the controller in slow motion. The start of a new chapter, the end of the old shitty one.

* * *

4 hours into a Rock n' Roll marathon Nancy's husband John, the human megaphone, yelled down into the basement.

"DINNER!!1!1"

We hadn't met John yet and frankly that was alright. He worked all day, came home, made his presence known, went to sleep, did it over again. He wore oval glasses and was bald on top. He looked like a bad guy, a villain. He looked like a serial killer. He scared me.

"LIGHTS OFF IN THE BASEMENT!"

We turned the bottoms of our socks black sliding around the house. We slid into the kitchen. I was starving. I wanted what the moms made. Italian gravies that turned you into an animal, found you a good 30 minutes before dinner was ready and hung there, slowly ramping up, layering in. Man.

I swung my legs back and forth under my chair. I was already comfortable here. My brother, too. He was smiling. We all were. Except John. John had to work all day so he didn't smile.

I didn't understand why John was there. What did he DO!? (he was an engineer) He seemed so out of place. While we all had fun he was missing. And when he was there he was so serious. His voice was deep and he projected it in a cone.

"LIGHTS OFF IN THE BASEMENT!"

We were ready to fork in the spagetti but there was still one empty seat at the table. It was Will, Michael's older brother. It was always Will. Probably finishing up a game. It was tough to get him to the table, he just wanted to play Warcraft all day. We watched him play sometimes, Warcraft was sweet.

* * *

Nancy spent an hour cleaning up, then their side peeled off to watch X-Files. The TV shone into the hallway, their backs to us, taking in all that stuff just before bed. I slowly walked by and peered in, caught a few seconds of a scene, an army General cutting into his scrambled eggs and deep red blood pouring out. That was it for me.

I got into my jammies. I was top bunk with a bunch of Michael's retired stuffed animals. I stared at them. They stared back. I looked away. I peered over the top of the frame and listened to my mom read us James and the Giant Peach. In this, a kid is abused by his Aunts but manages to escape out to sea, from England to N Y C, and make friends with a bunch of oddballs. With my mom reading, the book played like a movie in my head, written by her and directed by me. She enunciated and did the voices. She did the Roald justice.

My mom could have put us to bed and poured herself a glass of wine, drank the whole bottle. She could have read a book of her own, or chatted with Nancy, or just sat in front of the TV, or taken the minivan out into the blizzard and drove herself into a ditch.

* * *

One second it was night time, the very next second it was morning. I'd turn on one side, close my eyes, and feel the weight of the day lift off the ground. As I drifted, a group of balloons were taking off and I grabbed onto them, floated up until I didn't feel anymore, and it was all black, and I was asleep.

I was awake again. My eyes opened to grey daylight that had soaked through the blinds. I remembered where I was. I climbed down the bunk, passed my brother who was still asleep, took little steps over to the window. I could tell there was snow out there from the amount of glare alone. The question was how much. I had a good feeling about it. I looked through the blinds.

I ran back over and collapsed into my brother's bed, shook him awake. Rocked him back and forth by his exposed arm. He stirred, moved his face away from his drool puddle, found himself.

The two of us summoned Michael. It was dark. All the other doors in the house were closed. It was just the three of us. We zipped up and went out the kitchen slider. Michael took the lead.

"And that's where Will fell off and had to get seven stitches in his arm."

"And that's where a deer died and we had to call to get it taken away."

My brother raised his arm and pointed.

"And what about that?"

"That's just a tree."

We plowed the snow in from different angles, stacked it up and packed it in. I packed some onto the side and my brother smoothed it out. Michael seemed to have the most practice. He coolly made a smooth wall on his side, then helped us with ours. He was one of these guys that would be quiet and do a good job. When we were all but finished Michael gave our structure a small window. It gave us an idea. We used sticks as rifles and played Soldiers. It was 2v1, with the 1 in the pillbox and the 2 trying to attack. But we couldn't agree on when our shots connected.

"I GOT YOU!"

"No, NO, it missed."

"I HIT YOU LIKE FIVE TIMES, YOU'RE DEAD."

"NO, NO, NOOOOOOOOOO—"

We shrugged it off, walked toward the treeline at the very back of the property. I looked into the woods, felt good. Aside from the blabber mouths, it was deafeningly quiet. I just wanted to be still.

Then my brother threw a grenade from downtown. It exploded against my collar and dripped down. He'd gone too far. You're not supposed to DO that. I turned to face him. I wanted to fully send it, take aim and go off like a ball machine. But I was overdressed and had the flexibility of the Tin Man. So I dropped to my knees, scooped one together and heaved it. My brother sidestepped it with ease. I did it again but used my whole body to send it this time. It soared, curved in. I swear, it even made a sound. It hit my brother like a bag of kitty litter. It was satisfying. Something I did actually worked. I had drawn blood. It made my brother competitive.

It was all out. The battle went from Revolutionary War style to Vietnam. We had traded shots but now all bets were off. We just started hurling it. It didn't look pretty but screw it, you were just trying to connect. But damn, I didn't have aim or velocity. I eventually found an opening and lobbed one in. It dropped on my brother's head like birdshit. Then he locked onto me, fired one into my ribs. I put my hand up just in time to block another. He closed in. It was over.

* * *

My mom was spread out at the kitchen table. She spent so much time poring over papers, usually while on the phone. The piles of papers multiplied and just kept extending out. Countertops and tables got covered with a kind of blight. She looked up from her sea of documents as I came back through the slider. I was Han returning to the Rebel base. Michael and my brother were still out there, battling Wampas.

I waved, peeled off my snowy layers down to my long johns. My socks were wet and hung off the end of my feet. I pulled them off, left them there for someone else. Bread and jelly were already out, I grabbed the peanut butter and made the Holy Trinity. A morning PB&J to fuel the day. Mom had some news.

"Hey, Eri..."

I made my sandwich but my mom would have to cut the crusts off.

I heard my mom talking, heard her say "karate". I looked over. KARATE?

Then suddenly imagined it: being an ace, kicking people off buildings, beating everybody up like Arnold from Terminator 2. I liked pretending to be other people, this was good. I bunched up the plastic on the loaf of bread and spun it around, tied it off.

In karate I didn't even earn yellow, the very first belt upgrade. I just did some hiyas and watched a guy with an overgrown mustache tell us what to do. I was four.

But there was also this weird thing at the gym, a random set of stairs with no purpose. It was just five or six steps that fell straight off. My brother called it the Stairs to Nowhere. This was the highlight. Karate was pointless but these stairs...we walked up and down, my brother in the lead. Nobody paid much attention. Not even mustache man minded. We were rulers of this place that nobody cared about. The Stairs to Nowhere, just like the basement, was ours.

It was cold and dark when karate ended. We packed into my mom's old minivan and drove back home.

When we got in, Will was playing Warcraft. It was an intense one versus one. We crowded around the monitor. It was Orcs vs Humans, Will played Orc. He was playing over the internet against some other kid who was sitting at his computer, hundreds of miles away.

I saw Will move his mouse and press something on his keyboard. His guys moved across the screen. They had green skin and mohawks. They threw spears. They roared and swung huge weapons. Orcs were awesome.

I later learned that the same people who made Warcraft made Rock n' Roll Racing. I wouldn't have been able to take the news at the time. I wouldn't have been able to understand it.

* * *

Dinner was always good but this one was different. My mom had to move her papers this time. The blight receded. It was an Italian/American/German feast celebrating the great relationship white Americans always had with indigenous people. It's a great story and one that we celebrated every November. Amen.

Right after Thanksgiving, Nancy surprised us with a Boston Cream Pie for my brother's 9th birthday. She walked it over to the ktichen table, sensitive little candy cane candles flickering to death. We were all feeling good but my brother stood out. His smile showed real teeth.

He was kneeling on his chair, leaning over the table into the Boston Cream Pie, his face orange from the light. He made a wish.

* * *

The snow kept coming. It was a real winter's winter, a boy's winter. It kept the snowball fights and the sledding and the treks into the back woods rolling. There were 5 boys in the house and we were all friends. I was the youngest by far but it didn't matter, we were all pals. Five, fifteen, whatever.

It was a time to remember and it only existed because our life was off the rails. We were off the trail and had found something hidden. The trail is shit, the jungle is everything.



1995 - I'm 5

I came into the kitchen and saw my mom making some cinnamon raisin toast. She didn't seem terribly happy. The table was clear. No more papers. Something was different but I wasn't sure what. I was freshly 5 so had to be told most things. My mom spread fake butter on her toast, saw I was there, said hello. She had 2 slices, gave me 1. We sat down to eat and she told me what was going on. I swung my legs back and forth under the chair. She laid it out that we were leaving soon.

We lived at Nancy's for months but there were adult issues sprouting up like phone bills, other bills. Maybe they couldn't support three extra people living in their house forever. We had overstayed our welcome so it was back on the road.

Back in '93 my mom inherited the family home in the divorce, something close to 3,000 square feet. Shortly after the divorce my mom rented it out for income. When you're on the road with your two kids, low on cash, desperate, people tend to care a lot. If you're in a big white house, people don't care. This was my mom's strategy: get people to care. In her eyes it was survival.

So we needed a place to live. There was no way it was going to top Nancy's but Nancy's was a gift, one we didn't deserve. Now it was back to reality.

My mom had something lined up that she wasn't crazy about. We drove there with a packed car. It felt like the old days when we'd go into New York City for my brother's commercials. Long road trips with my mom at the wheel, my brother in the front, me in the middle.

We ended up pulling into a shelter in Flemington, New Jersey.

This place was loaded with women fleeing their husbands, all of whom were more desperate than us. But we managed to slide in. The house was big, with a kitchen for group meals and a random volley ball net outside by the road, over some blacktop. As poor as it seemed it was all we had so we used it.

My brother set up the Super Nintendo. I watched him play Zelda, Donkey Kong, and a bunch of the obscure ones like Super Battle Tank 2. I could watch my brother play single player games for hours. I was no good so he played. He was great. I sat next to him as he smashed the buttons. The screen lit up our faces. When the game started, we were gone—sitting in cheap crap chairs, my brother controlling his character, me asking him questions about the game, he only sometimes answering—eyes glazed over, mouth slightly open like an old person at a nursing home. It crushed reality. We were taking a hard pass on that.

Ocasionally we'd spot a spider the size of a golf ball ball blast across the floor. I don't know how we slept. One advantage of the top bunk was maybe they'd attack my brother first and I could escape through the ceiling.

* * *

We were in this place for 90+ days. The first 60 went pretty fast. Time flies when you spend most of it in your head. But my mom was fighting for an extension. God help us. Was this really the best we could do? I continued to watch my brother play Zelda: A Link to the Past. If only it linked us to Nancy's. My brother went on playing Super Nintendo while I watched. We both waited to be older. Surely it got better. Cooler stuff, more freedom. My brother whacked pixelated bushes to see what was inside, dodged a slow moving arrow. He disappeared inside a building that had beautiful harp music playing. We just stayed in the room and listened for a while. Who made this? They were out there somewhere, living the life. I wanted to be one.

We eventually got hungry and went looking for the kitchen where several of the ladies were having lunch. They had prepared many boxes of Kraft Mac n Cheese, the skinny tube macaroni, and put it all in a ginormous white bowl. And on a big plate to the side, boiled hot dogs. The mac n cheese was for people who weren't going to touch the sauerkraut. Like us kids/sane people. It probably would have done me some good but it stank and looked gross. I played it safe. I shoveled in the Kraft Mac like it was my ticket out of there.

There were toddlers at the shelter, too. Children of single moms and most likely arsehole fathers. We never met any of the fathers, just the moms. That was the point of this place. These women had no money and their only friends lived at the shelter. You look at their kids and figure they have no chance. But then you think the same about yourself and realize it's not so bad. Maybe they became artists or therapists. Maybe they became drug addicts and murderers. All depends on which way the wind is blowin.

After our 30 day extension we left the shelter and were gone from that place for good.

* * *

We were on the road again. Me in the back again. The slider door had a mind of its own and would randomly lock and then unlock, over and over. We were sure we had a ghost. What we really had was a shitty old van.

It wasn't a long drive this time. Roughly what it took to get to the mall. I didn't know where we were but it had to be better than the last one. We pulled into a small parking lot off route 206. It looked normal. Two story building, brown. It wasn't an office but it wasn't a house either. I thought the whole thing was going to be ours. I opened the slider door, jumped out, saw the great pine trees that were there. My brother broke off, I followed. My mom knew she had lost us, we were no good in an administrative engagement anyway. We looked up at the trees. They were perfect. My brother scaled first, me a good 2 or 3 branches below. Suddenly we were 15, 20 feet up. We were completely hidden. We could have stayed up there long term. We thought about it.

We called this guy Willy the Wiggly. He gave us the keys. For some reason we moved a bunch of our shit in from storage instead of finding a furnished place. As if we were going to stay. It made no sense, but here we were. None of it made sense but it was fun enough. Better than same old same old. It was life at the back of a whipping tail.

Our 17-inch TV went on a plastic thing with wheels that might have cost 50 cents. We set up the VCR so we could have something to do at night. We put in The Terminator. My mom didn't want us watching Braveheart but The Terminator was OK...????...It was all the Arnie scenes, how his body slowly got worse and worse as the movie went on. That scene at the police station when he just kept coming, blowing people away...or when he poked his own eye out because it was so mutilated it would have given him away as a robot.

My brother and I sat there in a cluttered mess and watched. We weren't doing anything else but watching. In 1995 you did 1 goddamn thing at a time. My mom was passed out on the couch, snoring like a wildebeast. The actual ruckus of the movie kept her asleep. If we shut it off she'd wake up. Did she even see this movie? It's WAY darker than Braveheart. Only later did I find out what she thought was so bad about Braveheart. It's the scene where young Mel Gibson finds the barn full of kids who were hanged. That's what pushed her over the edge. Not the part where he took an animal horn and ran it through the English dude's neck.

We spent the Summer there. The highlight was always when my dad came to pick us up for the weekend. He worked a corporate job. His ID badge was on a retractable thing on his waist. He looked good. He was in his prime. We went to the movies and saw stuff that was over my head. Stuff he and even my brother liked but I had no clue. I couldn't follow movies, I just absorbed them at face value. I never followed the plot or knew what the hell was going on but I watched every second of every one. Except for The Terminator and some other ones. I followed those well enough. It was about the main character. I needed to know what he was after.

In our time off, my brother and I climbed the pine trees. No matter where we were, we were real kids together. We were eager and didn't give a crap about what was going on. All this stuff was my mom's problem. We couldn't help her anyway, we were dead weight. So we just did our thing. It didn't matter what the circumstances were, we made a path.

Later that Summer we found a rental in Hopewell, New Jersey—the best town in all. It was an actual town. It was very old so had kind of a European vibe. Business was small, everything was. It didn't have a lot but it had it all. We were about to find out.

I opened the back slider door of my mom's old Plymouth and hopped out. The doors clanked shut one by one. We were standing before our new home, a real aged majesty. Our landlord, an old guy who looked like he could go at any minute but still wore a big smile, appeared out of the front door to greet us. He chatted with my mom while I hit a skinny stick against the ground. I hit the stick into the ground over and over, same spot, slowly making a little hole in the dirt. Then he showed us inside.

It was an 18th century duplex, with bone dry floorboards that whined everytime you took a step. There was no sneaking up on anyone. There were downsides to the place like the attic, it was pretty scary. It was also buggy. So many bugs but none helping with the rent. Still, this place was like a little piece of home we broke off from Nancy's that was finally catching up with us.

We walked through each room, downstairs then upstairs. Our guy knocked on the wall, telling us we shared that with our neighbor. We shared the yard, too. It was enough space and it was ours, for now. As a duplex, it had character. It was asymmetrical. A little Tim Burton.

The old guy, still smiling, still very old, handed us the keys. It was time for us to move our crap in. My brother and I went straight for the Super Nintendo—the box that we packed ourselves that said SNES in our handwriting, TOYS AND GAMES in our mom's. Toys and games? Toys and games could be anything. It was EVERYTHING. We had to be more specific. The TV was 1,000,000,000 pounds but with the two of us we somehow managed it. My brother reached into the SNES box and grabbed the F-connector, what we used to hook the Nintendo up to the TV. With an F-connector you have to guide this pin-sized male end into a milimeter sized female end. Then screw it on the rest of the way. It was hard. My brother was good at it.

We put the Rock n' Roll Racing cartridge into the Nintendo and turned the TV to channel 3. If 3 showed static we messed something up/were screwed, but if we got a dark blueish screen with some noise and blips, we were good to go. I held my breath. We got it. We got the blueish screen.

* * *

I was entered into Kindergarten and my brother 4th grade. Hopewell really had it. People were nice. I got along with everyone. We loved the same things and didn't fight. There was nothing to fight over yet. We enjoyed the milk while someone else tended to the cow.

The people in your town were your friends and your whole world. The people the next town over didn't even exist.

The air smelt like fresh cut grass and moist dirt. I breathed it in, got a good pull, rose up into the air 15, 20 feet, glided. Life was a painting. This is what life is supposed to be.

Every morning I walked to school, every afternoon I walked home. Down North Greenwood Avenue, up a hill and over the steep bridge where the train tracks ran underneath. The trains weren't running at the time but they could have been. They should have been. It would have been the cherry on top. It was only about a mile to school but you passed so much on the way. To live a mile from everything, to walk everywhere. These are invaluable qualities of life.

I walked with a couple of friends. They had nice hair and wore nice clothes. They smiled a lot, showing off their dimples. They played soccer. They grew up and changed their names to Norman, moved to Boring, and shat themselves to sleep. But for now they were my friends. They stopped and got a bagel with cream cheese every day. I got nothing every day. I waited for them and we walked the rest of the way to school.

* * *

Hopewell people extracted the flavors of the month. The real test was when old people cared about young people stuff, like Halloween. Years later Halloween would feel like a formality but in 1995 it was a real thing. There was authenticity back then. Things had meaning. Meaning exists when people say it does, and most people wanted there to be a lot of meaning so there was...

Big swaths of little goblins ran through the street, pumpkin candy buckets flying. Every house had something else going on, there wasn't a single dark one in the whole town. People existed, in flesh and blood. Even the grownups had fun. They wanted to participate. They unleashed their kids into the night and stayed behind to serve other people's kids. In the weeks and months leading up, they had gone overboard with decorations and spooky booby traps, awesome stuff like severed bodies spilling out from under the house, an arm reaching out to grab you.

I ran up to the door, stepped on the welcome mat between two costumed statues. I rang the bell. FDKNGERNERNGEGNKDHGNKDMSDLMG. The statues moved. I flew backwards onto my ass. The statues looked at each other. A lady, dressed normal, opened the door and gave me an extra piece of candy.

I had to be more careful. The town was alive, crawling with one-night performances. We had signed up for this haunted house when we put the mask on. Candy came at a cost. If I weren't so constipated I'd have shit my pants.

Burton Ave. I was blocks away from home, in parts of the town I had never seen. It was barely 8:00 but I had scowered Heaven and Hell, shown off my costume to millions. Time was being stretched from a dough. Minutes here were like hours on any other good day. The night would go on forever, and yet it would all be over far too soon.

I was focused on the here and now. I was dressed up for Halloween but wasn't pretending to be someone else. I was already the best I would ever be. I didn't know it but didn't need to. I thought we were all OK. Actually, I didn't even think about it.

I ran from house to house with my pillowcase, dropping candy in one by one. Trampling the yards. Cape flowing behind.



1996

In Kindergarten you were either AM or PM. I was PM. I radared in to the table that had a winter scene set up and a bunch of toy cars. A little girl joined in but she didn't know how to play. People moved the cars too fast, or raised them in the air and like flew them around. WHAT ARE YOU DOING. I had to leave. I went over to the carpet, sat down, spread my legs, and moved my feet in a windshield wiper motion. I did this for more than 20 minutes. Then my friend Gus joined in. We talked about cartoons.

My mom took us to Disney World for three full weeks in December. Most parents would never do this but my mom knew what was important. I remember Disney more than two extra weeks of Kindergarten.

Everyone knows about Disney and it sucks now, so I'm not going to talk about it. Yea yea, Space Mountain. I found Hershey Park to be way better. It was less than an hour away. The only problem with Hershey is that I was on the ride where they show you how the chocolate's made and I dropped my candy. It was an epic multicolored piece I had barely touched. I cried. It ruined the ride.

After three weeks at Disney I returned to Kindergarten with a fresh coat of paint. I was a new man. I was the king of the room when I walked in. After three weeks I felt two years older. I walked into class with gelled hair, sunglasses and a blazer. I moved a toothpick between my teeth. I looked at kids confidently. I shot them with a finger pistol. I had stepped away for a second and it gave me something. I was ready for anything. It was time for show and tell.

* * *

I knelt on the couch and looked out the window, barely able to see the street through the chonkiness of the flakes. Like they were cut out of construction paper. God's bad dandruf, the angels' intense pillow fight, etc. The type that started sticking almost immediately because the ground was so cold. The snow just started piling up.

Any old dusting was cool but a blizzard blew the top straight off. It's because we knew school was going to be closed. You couldn't plow this. It's like wiping your hands after every rib. Let it go, let it pile up. Close the schools. Close everything. Find your new purpose in the snow. And we did. The recess was forming right outside the window. I was in art class with Mrs. Sprout. Mrs. Sprout looked like a gargoyle. Many wrinkles, a lot of makeup, and very thin. Her skin just kind of hung off in parts. Her eyes were engulfed in a blue makeup, her lips a bright red. Her jewelery clanked around her wrist, hung long off her neck.

Mrs. Sprout was very theatrical about everything. She had to have lived alone, in terms of humans, and at least eight cats. She sounded like she smoked but I don't think she did. I looked down at my art, which was shit. We were all getting some lesson on Jackson Pollock's splatter paintings.

This one kid, Josh, a real asshole, and clown, wasn't doing a lick of work. He was leaning back in his chair, on its hind legs, like you weren't supposed to. He did it all the time and never did work. He was almost two years older than everyone else in the class. The back of his head was flat, like his brain was forgotten. He was a complete idiot and disruptive, too. Now he was going off on Pollock.

"I could do that, I could do that," he kept saying. "I don't get it, an eight year old could do it."

But you didn't Josh. You didn't. And you couldn't. I thought about Josh just melting into lava. So we wouldn't have to deal with him ever again, in any of our classes. Somehow I was stuck with him all the time. Him and his friends were some of the worst. I imagined them all living together in a truck trailer and eating sticks. Shitting themselves and eating that, too.

Mrs. Sprout was nice, though. She gave some de-escalating response. Everyone knew Josh by now and it wasn't worth fighting him. It's all he wanted. He just wanted to watch the world burn. But dammit he was too stupid to be interesting. His world burning would be incredibly lame.

The classroom phone rang. Mrs. Sprout instructed us to keep working and walked over to where the phone sat, right by the front door. She picked it up. I was looking at her. I looked at her as she went from happy Mrs. Sprout to Mrs. Sprout that just got punched in the face. Her lips moved and said:

"Oh my god..."

She nodded. She had just gotten the news of her life and it wasn't good. She put the phone back on the receiver, put her hand to her mouth, and slowly re-entered the class. I had watched her the whole time. Now what?

She gave us the news as best she could. An airplane had struck the Twin Towers. New York City was under attack.

It was like Pearl Harbor or something. Every single person was talking about it. The footage was on every TV. Not just one plane, but two. Both towers were hit and after an hour of being engulfed in flames, went down. I couldn't really understand it, but it was obviously really bad. How could this happen? What was REALLY happening?

I got home and the footage was being replayed on our small white kitchen TV. Over and over. My mom was on the phone. I kept watching it as a small black dot zoomed into the frame, flew straight into the second tower. And then a huge explosion. This wasn't like the movies at all, there was something visceral about it. You knew all those people instantly died, in the plane and in the building. The flames were real, everything was. It was hard not to keep watching. It was the moment of our lives and nothing would be the same ever again. Something was switched on, or off, probably off. The earth had been lobotomized in a way. We didn't feel it yet but something was definitely lost forever. A kind of innocence we all were taking for granted, that we were living with for decades.

For months the 9/11 attacks were all that anyone talked about and all that was on TV. Again and again the footage was played. Not one plane, but two. One could have been a mistake, but then the second one came in. It was horrible. Footage of people jumping out of the building to avoid burning to death. What they must have thought on the way down...

There were stories circulating of people who worked in the buildings, who got a flat tire or were sick that day. We weren't very far from the city, just an hour drive in. This felt real. It was real. Something truly God awful happened on September 11th, 2001 and we were all still processing it. Would continue to process it for years. No one could really believe it. We had been living under a rock, inside a bubble, wrapped in warm blankies. The real world had been shielded from us.

Not anymore.

* * *

Everybody was still wearing 9/11 but we had to shake it off at some point. The holidays were a pretty good opportunity to get distracted.

At Christmas my mom went all out. Our house was like The North Pole. We set up a snow village so big it had its own zip code. Fake snow flowed down the stairs and wrapped around the banister. You found it in your hair. The tree was huge and decorated with a thousand ornaments and enough lights to take down the power grid. My mom and I spent most of the time doing the decorating and setting up, my brother was less interested.

We even had a choo-choo train doing laps. It ran through the village as if the people could get off and on. The place was on fire. The carols were going and the eggnog was flowing. My mom seemed permanently tied up in the kitchen. Her classic chocolate-vanilla swirl cake was a hit, as well as her oatmeal-chocolate-chip cookies—grab'em while they're hot. They fell apart in our hand, we lapped up the crumbs. We were animals.

Christmas morning was a site to behold. I don't know how she did it. Under the tree were the two longest strips of presents I had ever seen. Nobody had Christmas like this. The presents extended from the tree all the way out into the other room. Me and my brother both had over 25 boxes of gifts EACH. I had never seen anything like it. We didn't deserve it but there it was. How did she wrap everything? Why did she get so much? Most kids would have been electrified with five or six gifts each, but 25...

My brother's boxes were red and mine were green. His were on the left and mine were on the right. We took our seats and posed for some photos. The photos kept coming, we kept posing. COULD WE JUST OPEN OUR GIFTS?

We got the green light. My mom was done snapping. One by one we tore in. Clothes, toys, video games—the works. Nothing was left out. We got everything we wanted and then some. There were a few mom gifts in there but you had to expect that.

Then we peeled open the best one, the only one that truly mattered once the dust settled: XBOX. And right beside it, Halo. A brand new console and a brand new game. I loved all my toys. I got the RC car I wanted, the big grey F-150. I got stuff I didn't even know I wanted. More GI-Joes, more everything. But Halo for XBOX was THE one. Me and my brother just weren't prepared for what was to come. Sometimes high expectations get passed like they're standing still.



2002

The story is the blood of a game, blood of anything, and Halo was one giant blood bag. The full title was Halo: Combat Evolved3, and goddamn was the combat evolved. But the story held it all together. It had both the epicness and intimacy of Star Wars, but was its own thing. It's 500 years in the future where humans finally have the technology to ramble through space, aliens exist and they're not our friends. There are other aliens that are less advanced and are definitely not our friends. So you fought aliens in space. The goal was to stop them from gaining control of a superweapon that could threaten humanity. But that's just the bare bones of it. It sounds stupid because a story is a whole lot more than that. It was a freaking videogame but made most 200 million dollar movies look like utter shit. It walked on everything, sat on it too. As soon as Halo hit the shelves it was the only one.

At 7AM my alarm went off and I went straight to the sock room. My brother was already in there, controller in hand. The XBOX booted up. Every time the game loaded you got the music, the space-operatic OOOOOOHHHHHHH OOOOOAWWHHHHH. The music was on another level.

To THINK that the campaign was this good and would still just become a small footnote on the overall legacy of the game. It didn't seem possible. But it was just a tasty morsel to fish you in. Once you were in that OK piece of French bread you were eating was now toasted and buttered and filled with...cheese. Yes. The campaign set the bar high but the multiplayer, where you played against each other, broke the ceiling.

* * *

My brother was on the portable phone. He laughed, hung up.

"Eric, Halo at Ben's house at 3:30."

"Cool," I said, but inside my geysers were exploding. I never organized anything, these were my brother's friends not mine. So it was up to him to make us both part of the group, to get the time and place, to include me. And he always did. He included me in everything.

The Street Hockey crew was also the Halo crew, or most of them. And wouldn't you know it, Ben was by far the best at Halo too. God, couldn't we have anything? Was Ben even organic? I pushed my finger into his arm to see. He smacked me in the chest with the back of his hand.

Halo at Ben's meant eight-person Halo. 4v4. You could play with up to four players on a single XBOX, and we did that plenty. The only problem was you could see other people's screen and know where they were on the map. It was cheating but everyone did it. I guess it gave everyone a maphack if you knew what you were doing, had the hang of the maps. Four-person Halo was great. It was good enough and we could go on playing it for eternity and be in Heaven. But 4v4 is what broke the ceiling. This was the highlight. 4v4 required TWO XBOXes. Which meant two TVs, two different rooms, and NO screen sharing. This was huge. It meant you really didn't know where your opponents were. You were together with your four-person team, on a single TV, going after the other four guys in the other room on their TV. You could strategize, you could play stealthy. Not to mention, shit man, it's just better with more players. Eight was the magic number.

The game was always at Ben's because he had the nicest house. It was huge and it was clean. The exterior was white brick, the interior was spotless white carpet. Classic Asian household. It smelled like weird Asian spices. Ben's mom yelled at him constantly. He would have to pause the game sometimes to talk with her in Chinese. We just prayed we could continue playing. I closed my eyes, said a little prayer. Please Lord may Ben own his mom in this argument, please Lord make Ben's mom go away. Please Lord make 4v4 Halo my eternal existence. Amen.

Ben re-emerged, said we could keep playing. More proof he was the best at everything. He repeatedly took the stand against his mom and sent her packing. Back to the couch, back to the kitchen, to Hell. Anywhere but here. JUST LET US PLAY OUR FUCKING GAME BITCH. Oh thank God.

Last year, in 5th, I was the star of the play so I went out again this year. I thought I'd be a shoe in for the lead but 1st year of middle school and so forth, bottom of the food chain, I got a small part. I dropped it. I was pissed. I turned my attention elsewhere. I started noticing girls. They didn't notice me. I was still working off my baby fat and couldn't look them in the eye. I was also uncool. Man, middle school really sucked ass. This town was a big pile of shit and middle school just made it worse. People were starting to get mean. No one had the maturity to rise above anything and all the hormones were piling in. I had to get the hell out of there. I was trapped in that sterile building. How is this what we created? Who would ever put their kids here?

It was another big push inward. I wasn't getting what I needed from the outside so had to go the other way. I'm so glad things sucked, though. It started to create something different inside, something I could use. Big cheers to the shitbag with the pink shirt and popped collar, always trying to beat people up. Extra special thanks to that clown Josh who stayed back two years and wouldn't shut the hell up in class. There were so many, I could never go through them all. I couldn't do them all justice. These people were made out of glass and plastic. They still are. I was sure I was better and that I didn't belong there.

2:30 hit. The dumb bell rang. I crashed through the doors. ADIOS. Oxygen. I ran to my bus. I was one of the first ones on. CAN WE START THIS THING. I looked out the window at the sea of kids finding their buses, too. I had to figure out a way to get through this. I had six more years of it. There had to be something in here somewhere, some kind of lubricant. Not drugs of course, but something. Something that wasn't a drug but was a drug. I'd find it. I'd move things along the surface until it fell into place and meshed seamlessly. People would think I was interested and engaged but I'd be on Saturn. I started writing a book.

* * *

I got in, threw my backpack off like it was a freaking monkey on my neck. It went flying. I floated up the stairs to my room. I had some kind of purpose. I just needed a fresh notebook. And if I didn't have one I'd get one. They were 99¢ tops. 99¢ could turn into $1M if you put the words in the right order. At the very least it would dig a tunnel between 6th grade and salvation. All I needed was one of those black and white marble sons of bitches and a pen. I didn't have a marble, I had a spiral. I used it. I turned to the first blank page, wrote TABLE OF CONTENTS. Bookmarked it for later. Flipped to several pages in, wrote "Chapter 1".

I started writing. The faces of all the crappy kids just melted away. I was smiling. My mouth was going to be sore tomorrow I was smiling so freaking big. The story just flowed. I was at my desk 20 minutes and had the first chapter written. It wasn't much but it was something. I flipped back in the book and wrote the title. Space Marines: 22874. That was it. This was going to be it.
